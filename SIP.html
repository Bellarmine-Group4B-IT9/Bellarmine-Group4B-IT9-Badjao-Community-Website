Background And Significance of the Study

By: Chenikandiyil

What is the value of composting to communities and the environment?

Composting benefits both communities and the environment by assisting in the breakdown of organic wastes such as leaves, grass, clippings, and kitchen waste. It includes several essential nutrients for plant growth such as micronutrients (zinc, manganese, iron, boron, etc.), as well as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and is hence commonly used as fertilizer. It also helps to enhance soil by retaining moisture and controlling plant diseases and pests. Composts enhance and balance the soil density through the addition of carbon and other plant nutrients like zinc, manganese, iron and etc. which allows plants to grow stronger roots into the soil, and promotes better growth. The addition of nutrients to the soil by compost increases the growth of fungus and other organisms, which in turn compete with or eliminate nematodes; this improves the general health of plants and reduces their susceptibility to pests. Since compost includes various essential components for plant growth, it also promotes increased agricultural crop yields. Compost can generally help reforestation, wetlands restoration, and habitat revitalization initiatives by enhancing dirty, compacted, and marginal soils. Composts aids in wetlands restoration by replacing essential organic material, allowing it to absorb up to four times its weight in water. Compost can also help with reforestation efforts by providing as an ideal growing medium for young seedlings. Similarly, compost can aid in the revegetation of habitats, providing the necessary sustenance for native wildlife populations.

How can the specific community or institution your PT group is engaged with, make use or benefit from composting in general, and from your group’s composting project specifically?

Our partner institution (BAMAI) can make use or benefit from composting in general by using it to plant plants/trees and make them grow and become healthier, which help solve and mitigate pollution by reducing the carbon dioxide in the air, and releasing cleaner and fresher air. Now, as we know, the BAMAI has its own problems with pollution (specifically garbage pollution), for there is so much garbage that is scattered across the shorelines of the Badjao community (which they take care of), and that is polluting the place. That is why, if BAMAI follows the practice of composting, or the concept of our group's composting project (The Effect of Banana Peels on the Height and Number of fruits of the Solanum Lycopersicum) in general (the use of banana peels as a compost)l, it will help them combat the pollution that they are experiencing in the shorelines of their Badjao community. This research is not only aiming for the benefit of the partner institution but also a way to resolve pollution in our and everyone's environment as well as an encouragement to plant more healthy trees and plants that also helps resolve deforestation. Other direct benefits of composting to BAMAI if they are unable to plant tomatoes include increased crop yields and increased understanding about nutrient-dense foods. Composting may also assist Badjaos in learning and practicing solid waste management, as well as in making their soil healthy, which they can use to make a living and produce various types of agricultural crops that they can consume and sell to provide the finest lived for themselves, a life with a sustainable income, and food. This research will tell us if it is beneficial for the community and environment; and the additional knowledge about the composting of banana peels.

SIP METHODOLOGY

By: Chenikandiyil

SIP Project Title "The Effect of Banana Peels as a compost, on the Height and Number of fruits of the Solanum Lycopersicum (Tomato plant)"
Problem What is the effect of adding Banana Peels to the height and number of fruits of Solanum lycopersicum (Tomato Plant)?
Hypothesis If Banana Peels are added to the soil of Solanum lycopersicum (Tomato plant) as a compost , then it will grow taller and bear more fruits.

VARIABLES

Independent Variable Banana Peels Compost
Dependent Variable/s Height and Number of Fruits of the Solanum Lycopersicum
Constant or Controlled Variables Same type of soil, same type/amount of water, same amount of sunlight exposure, same temperature given, same pot size, same amount of soil, same tomato plant, starting size of plant

TREATMENTS / SET-UP

Control Set-up Experimental Set-up 1
This set-up is tomato seeds that are growing WITHOUT the addition of Banana Peels as a compost. This set-up is tomato seeds that are growing WITH the addition of Banana Peels as a compost.
3 trials or replicates. 3 trials or replicates.

EXPERIMENTAL DATA TO BE MEASURED OR OBSERVED

Experimental Measurement or Observation Description of Measurement or Observation Significance of Observation
Height of the Solanum Lycopersicum in Inches. At the end of every week, for 4 weeks straight, the individual heights of the 3 tomato plants/seeds in the Control set-up will be measured, calculated, and will be totalled , and the same thing will be done with the 3 tomato plants/seeds in the Experimental set-up.

But, at the end of all the 4 weeks (when the experiment is done), get the separate averages of all of the total heights of the plants in the control set up, and the plants in the experimental set up, then compare which group has a higher average height.

Note: We will measure the height of the plant by its stem (in cm), so from the soil, wherever the stem is visible, you will measure, to its highest point.		 It is one of the Dependent variables; it is one of the basic determinants of whether or not a plant is healthy because when compared to another plant, the height the plants can tell you which one is healthier/growing better; the taller the plant the more nutrients it is receiving.
Number of of fruits/tomatoes that the Solanum Lycopersicums (tomato plants) produce. At the end of all the 4 weeks (when the experiment is done), get the separate averages of all of the total heights of the plants in the control set up, and the plants in the experimental set up, then compare which group has a higher average height. Do this also to the number of tomato fruits of the plants in the control set-up, then with the experimental set-up. Then compare which set-up has a higher average of height and fruits produced. It is one of Dependent variables; a very solid basis of whether or not the plant is healthy/growing well, because it is/not producing fruit.

PROCEDURES FOR TESTING AND GATHERING/OBTAINING DATA

Step-by-Step Procedure

Producing a Compost Material (Banana peels)

1. Chop 0.30 grams of banana peels into smaller pieces.

2. Apply the loam soil to the pots (Just enough to fill the pot, but not too full, follow the line guide on your pots if there are)

3. Put or sprinkle small pieces of 0.10 grams of banana peels on top of each pot (soil), to help speed up the composting process.

4. Sprinkle/spray 40ml of water to make the banana peels damp/moist and not dry.

5. Leave the banana peels in the soil for about a week.

Applying Compost to the Tomato Plant Process:

1. Wear gloves to avoid dirt

2. Prepare the 3 pots with the soil and compost that was prepared in for the experimental set-up

3. Then, prepare another 3 pots for controlled set-up

4. Apply the loam soil to the 3 pots for the controlled set-up (Just enough to fill the pot, but not too full, follow the line guide on your pots if there are)

5. Plant the 6 tomato seeds (1 seed per pot) inside the soil, about ¼ inch deep in each pot

6. Water the plants with 100ml of water so that the soil will moist

7. After 2 weeks, mix the compost and soil together, from the pot

8. Do the watering/spraying (100ml) once a day for 4 weeks straight

Making Observations and/or Measurements about the Compost

We will be making observations and/or measurements about the compost and/or compost soil mixture, those observations will be seeing if the Banana Peels are composting well while keeping them exposed to air (for the most part), and while keeping them moist. Since those are 2 things that help/speed up the composting process. the overall appearance/quality of the Banana Peels during its composting process, and during the growth and observation period of the tomato plants, will be observed (just to make sure that it is composting properly/well).

Dosage and Frequency of Compost Application to the Test Plants

We will be putting 0.10 grams whole chopped Banana Peel per plant/pot. We will only be applying the Banana Peel Compost once, meaning to say we will just put it on/in the soil of the tomato plants in the experimental setup once, and leave it with them. But remember, we will first be sprinkling the Banana peels on top of the soil of the tomato plants, then after a 2 weeks we will be mixing it with their soil.

Making observations and/or measurements about the Test Plants

Experimental Set-up

1. After the first week of planting and adding the compost on top of the tomato plant’s soil, take a measuring tape to measure all of the tomato plant's height (in cm), and record it. Then total them up, and record.

2. Next count the total number of fruits that all of the tomato plants produced, and record it, then total them and record.

3. Repeat this process every week for 4 weeks STRAIGHT.

Controlled Set-up

1. After the first week of planting the plants/seeds, take a measuring tape to measure all of the tomato plant's height (in cm), and record it. Then total them up, and record

2. Next count the total number of fruits that all of the tomato plants produced, and record it, then total them and record.

3. Repeat this process every week for 4 weeks STRAIGHT.

NOTE: At the end of all the 4 weeks (when the experiment is done), get the separate averages of all of the total heights of the plants in the control set up, and the plants in the experimental set up, then compare which group has a higher average height. Do this also to the number of tomato fruits of the plants in the control set-up, then with the experimental set-up. Then compare which set-up has a higher average of height and fruits produced.
